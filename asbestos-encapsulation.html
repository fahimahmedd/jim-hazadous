Not all asbestos can be safely removed and in some instances, it makes more sense to leave it where it is and simply seal off the harmful material.

Asbestos is only a concern when it is broken apart, allowing asbestos threads to become airborne. Encapsulation is the process of coating the asbestos with a sealant which penetrates the asbestos, hardening the fibers and preventing the release of asbestos dust.

Two Types of Encapsulation

A bridging encapsulant and a penetrating encapsulant are used for different purposes.

Bridging Encapsulant

A bridging encapsulant serves to bridge the fibers over the surface, creating a protective barrier that prevents fiber release. Ideal for surfaces that won't be disturbed and where a durable coating is needed.

Best for: Pipe insulation, boiler lagging, wall panels

Penetrating Encapsulant

A penetrating encapsulant penetrates the material. The penetrating encapsulant also binds its components so there isn't any dangerous fallout. It hardens the fibers from within.

Best for: Friable asbestos, ceiling textures, vermiculite

The Science Behind Encapsulation

When asbestos fibers are bound together with a penetrating encapsulant, the sealant soaks into the material and chemically binds the fibers, preventing them from becoming airborne. The bridging encapsulant adds an additional layer of protection by creating a physical barrier over the surface.

Key Points to Remember:
